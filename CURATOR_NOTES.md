# Curator Notes: Marvel Comic Book Museum Transformation

My process for converting the design-centric website into a Marvel comic book museum involved a comprehensive overhaul of the site's content and visual identity.

First, I analyzed the existing file structure, which included `index.html`, `style.css`, and `prompt-pack.html`.

The core of the transformation was the `index.html` file. I replaced the existing content about design movements with a historical overview of Marvel Comics. This new content was structured into three distinct "eras":

1.  **The Golden Age (1939-1950s):** Focusing on the origins with Timely Comics, the creation of iconic characters like Captain America, and the influence of World War II. I highlighted key figures such as Martin Goodman, Joe Simon, and Jack Kirby.
2.  **The Silver Age (1961-1970s):** This section details the creative explosion led by Stan Lee and Jack Kirby, which gave birth to the Fantastic Four, Spider-Man, and the Avengers, introducing more complex and relatable superheroes.
3.  **The Modern Age (1980s-Present):** Here, the focus shifts to darker, more intricate storylines from writers like Chris Claremont and Frank Miller, the rise of major crossover events, and the eventual launch of the Marvel Cinematic Universe.

To match the new theme, I completely rewrote the `style.css` file. The new design incorporates a classic comic book aesthetic, using a bold color palette dominated by Marvel's signature red. I also integrated comic-style fonts and visual effects like text shadows and blocky borders to evoke the look and feel of a printed comic book.

Finally, I removed the `prompt-pack.html` file, as its content was no longer relevant to the new Marvel-themed site.

The result is a thematically consistent and engaging website that serves as a virtual museum exhibit on the history of Marvel Comics.

Swiss Style/ International Typographic Style:

Swiss Style, or International Typographic Style, emerged in 1950s Switzerland from schools like Basel and Zurich, prioritizing objectivity through sans-serif type, grids, and asymmetry. Pioneers Josef Müller-Brockmann and Armin Hofmann used flush-left rags, white space, and photography for clear communication in posters and books. It spread globally via Helvetica font, influencing corporate identity and modernism into the 1970s. Rejecting ornament, it stressed “legibility above all.”

Vocabulary Tags:
Typographic grid, Sans-serif, Asymmetric layout, Flush-left, Objectivity

Real Example Links
• Museum für Gestaltung Zürich Graphics: https://museum-gestaltung.ch/en/graphic-design
• Swiss Design Collection: https://museum-gestaltung.ch/en/exhibition/swiss-design-collection

Not to be confused with Bauhaus because Swiss Style focused on strict typographic grids and sans-serifs for print in post-WWII Switzerland, while Bauhaus integrated broader crafts and architecture pre-1933.

I verified this by checking Museum für Gestaltung Zürich and Flintridge Design History.

Reaction to Swiss:

Grunge Typography emerged in the early 1990s as a punk-fueled backlash against Swiss Style’s rigid grids and pristine sans-serifs. Designers like David Carson layered distorted fonts, photocopy textures, and chaotic overlaps in Ray Gun magazine, embracing illegibility for raw emotion. Fueled by Seattle grunge music and desktop publishing, it favored distressed edges and handwritten scrawls through the late 1990s. This anti-corporate rebellion prioritized attitude over order, influencing zines and album art.

Vocabulary Tags
Distressed texture, Deconstruction, Layered hierarchy, Dingbat overlay, Photocopy artifact

Real Example Links
• Cooper Hewitt Grunge Typography: https://collection.cooperhewitt.org/ (search “David Carson Ray Gun”)
• MoMA Design Collection: https://www.moma.org/collection/terms/ (search “grunge typography 1990s”)

Not to be confused with Postmodernism because Grunge specifically rejected Swiss precision via gritty, music-driven distortion in the 1990s, while Postmodernism broadly played with historical quotes earlier.

I verified this by checking The Verge and PRINT Magazine archives.
